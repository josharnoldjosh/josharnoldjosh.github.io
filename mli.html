A comparison between “The River” by Parkway Drive and “Gymnopédies N.o 1” by Erik Satie

￼#music/IA￼



Introduction

One of the most beautiful aspects of music is how a single idea or emotion can be expressed in a myriad of ways. This investigation explores the links between two distinct pieces, The River by Parkway Drive and Gymnopédies N.o 1 by Erik Satie, both of which convey the emotions of sadness and sorrow through contrasting mediums.


The River is a song composed and released in 2012 by the Australian metal core band, ParkwayDrive. The song is said to be written about Dylan Etherington, who was a close friend of Parkway Drive and produced a documentary for the band. Etherington died in 2010 after jumping off a 20 meter high bridge into the Neretva River after being warned about the dangers of jumping during winter. The piece is a medium for the band to mourn the loss of their friend. The River primarily employs the use of heavily distorted electric and bass guitars, screaming vocals, all played in B minor.


In contrast, the Gymnopédies N.o 1 was published in 1888 in Paris by the French composer, Erik Satie. At the beginning of the score, the Gymnopédies N.o 1 states the music is to be played “Lent et douloureux”, which translates directly to “slow and sorrowful”. However, unlike The River, Gymnopédies N.o 1 is a piano concerto that is regarded as being immensely relaxing, especially in comparison to The River.


Despite the difference in time period and origin, both pieces share musical links. This investigation will explore the following links between the two pieces; mild dissonances, simple but vague harmonies, and modal aspects.



Link 1: Mild dissonances

Both The River and Gymnopédies N.o 1 make use of mild dissonances to evoke a melancholy atmosphere.


Although The River is prominently an aggressive piece, it is framed between three contrasting sections—the introduction, the first interlude, and the second interlude—that are much calmer, featuring a clean, undistorted guitar, a female choir and the absence or reduction of the drums. The identification of contrasting sections is an important aspect to note when analysing The River as different techniques are employed in each section. The calmer sections of the piece are the most melancholic, while the aggressive sections are more modal.
